Operating Under Constraint: Job Search With MS

Where I Started

There was a company restructure. The initial momentum of a job search — the urgency, the structure and discipline it forces on you — carried me for a bit. Then it slowed. Follow-ups not following up. A market that was tougher than I originally expected, with more competition and fewer responses than the volume of applications.

That alone is demoralizing in the specific, cumbersome way that a long search gets demoralizing. But there was something else.

MS doesn’t pause for circumstances. Cognitive fatigue doesn’t check whether you have a recruiter screen before deciding to show up. Some days the working memory just isn’t there — reading the same job description or documentation three times and retaining less each time, losing the thread mid-sentence on something you’ve said dozens of times before, sitting down to prep and finding that mental sharpness just isn’t there. Not broken. Not incapacitated. But not sharp either, and sharpness is what interviews reward.

The search wasn’t happening in a vacuum. It was happening inside a body with its own schedule, running alongside a market with its own indifference. That combination required a different approach — not because I was trying to compensate, but because the standard approach wasn’t viable. Burning through limited cognitive reserves on memorization isn’t a strategy. It’s just expensive and fragile.

The Shift

Early in the search I was preparing the way most candidates do: rehearsing answers, listing technologies, anticipating questions. It’s a reasonable starting point but a poor finishing point.

When I picked up that a team ships roughly three to four major releases per year, I didn’t just log or store that, I planned around it. Low release frequency means high-stakes releases. High-stakes releases mean testing confidence is critical. That means QA isn’t just a speed function — it’s a risk management function. The team isn’t optimizing for shipping fast; they’re optimizing for not breaking things when they do ship.

That single inference changed how I framed every technical answer. I stopped talking about test coverage as output and started talking about it as signal quality. The question isn’t whether tests exist — it’s whether they tell you something trustworthy at the moment you need to make a decisive call.

How I Used AI in Prep

I want to be specific here because “I used AI to prep” can mean anything from generating a script to actually sharpening your reasoning.

I used it as a devil’s advocate, not a ghostwriter. I’d describe my positioning and ask where it was weak. I’d draft a technical answer and ask what a skeptical interviewer would push back on. I’d lay out an assumption I was making about the role and ask whether there was evidence to actually support it.

The goal was to be challenged before the interview, not validated. Validation is useless in this context. What I needed was to find the gaps in my reasoning while I still had time to close them.

This mattered more than it might for someone without cognitive variability in the picture. On a good day I can hold a complex technical narrative in my head, stress-test it internally, and identify where it’s thin. On a harder day that process is slower and less reliable — things that feel coherent in the moment turn out to have gaps I didn’t catch. Using an external tool to test my thinking meant I wasn’t depending entirely on my own working memory to find the flaws.

The structure it helped most with was separating facts from interpretations. I had real data points — the release cadence, some signals from the job description, things that came up in the hiring manager conversation. AI helped me be rigorous about which conclusions those data points actually supported versus which ones I was filling in from assumption. That discipline — treating delays as normal rather than rejection, not over-interpreting recruiter behavior — was something I had to work on explicitly. MS fatigue and a long search are the perfect combination for spiraling on ambiguous signals. Having a structured way to check my reasoning helped interrupt that.

The Technical Positioning

When I talked about my technical work, I wasn’t positioning myself as someone who writes tests. I was positioning myself as someone who improves feedback systems.

The distinction is concrete. I reduced CI runtime from 13–15 minutes down to 2–5 minutes through test sharding and smarter parallelization. That’s not just a testing improvement. It changes how quickly engineers get feedback after a change, which changes how they work, which changes how many small errors make it further downstream than they should.

I talked about API-first testing strategy as a way to get durable, fast signal without the fragility of full end-to-end tests for every case. I talked about CI as a feedback loop with a latency problem, not a quality gate that you bolt on at the end.

The framing I kept coming back to: automation is an engineering discipline, not a support function. The infrastructure around software development is as worth designing carefully as the software itself.

What Was Happening in the Background

While I was doing this preparation, I was also managing logistics that most candidates don’t have to think about.

Accessibility considerations for an in-office interview. Optimizing interview timing — late morning is usually my best cognitive window, so that’s what I asked for. Thinking through how much to disclose, and when, without either oversharing early or creating surprises later.

These aren’t complaints. They’re constraints. And like any engineering constraint, they required design decisions rather than just effort. You don’t push through a resource limitation by trying harder — you navigate around it intelligently and efficiently.

The energy management piece in particular affected how I structured prep sessions. I couldn’t do four-hour marathon preparation blocks. What I could do was focused, well-scoped sessions with clear objectives and real stopping points. That limitation forced a kind of preparation discipline that I think actually produced better results than brute-force rehearsal would have.

Now

I’m not treating anything as the finish line. It’s another data point in a process, and the outcome is partly outside my control. What’s inside my control is the quality of my thinking, the clarity of my communication, and the accuracy of how I represent what I’ve actually built and learned.

The search isn’t over. But something has shifted in how I approach interviews — and honestly, how I think about positioning technical work more generally. Reading systems rather than memorizing answers is a transferable skill. It’s one I’ll keep using regardless of how this particular process ends.

From the “look at it and figure out what’s wrong,” from my parent when I was younger and would take electronic appliances apart to figuring MS out – what works and what doesn’t work.

Progress doesn’t always announce itself. Sometimes it’s just: you’re thinking differently than you were four weeks ago, and that counts.
